# 라노벨 — Ranobel 

Learn Korean by reading the stories you already want to read.

---

## The Problem With Language Apps

Most Korean learning tools pull you out of what you're doing. You're reading a novel, you hit a word you don't know, you stop, you look it up, you lose your place, you lose your flow. Flashcard apps drill vocabulary in a vacuum. You memorize words but never see them in context.

Ranobel does the opposite. It brings Korean into the story — quietly, gradually, so you absorb the language without breaking your reading rhythm.

---

## How It Works

You read a novel chapter by chapter. Korean words appear inline, woven into the English text. Early chapters have just a few. Later chapters have many. By the end of a long series, a significant portion of what you're reading is Korean — and you won't need to look anything up, because you've already seen each word dozens of times.

**Early chapters** — a handful of common words appear with their romanization. Hover (or tap) any word to see a tooltip: the Korean script, the word type, a translation, and an example sentence in context.

**Mid-series** — words you've seen repeatedly drop the example sentence. The tooltip stays, but slimmed down. You're starting to just *know* these words.

**Late chapters** — familiar words shed the romanization entirely. You read the Korean characters directly, the way a reader would.

The progression tracks word frequency across the whole novel. Nothing is random. The system knows which words you've encountered and how often, and it adjusts what level of support to show accordingly.

---

## What You See When You Read

Korean words are color-coded by grammatical role — verbs in amber, nouns in blue, particles in pink, pronouns in green. This isn't decoration. It trains your eye to recognize sentence structure as you read, not after.

Particles get special treatment. Korean grammar leans heavily on particles (은/는, 이/가, 을/를 and many others) to show how words relate to each other. Shadow Diary highlights them inline with labels so you build an intuition for Korean sentence structure without ever opening a grammar textbook.

---

## Progress, Saved

Your reading position is saved locally. Come back tomorrow, pick up exactly where you left off. The bookmark button on any chapter locks your place.

---

## The Annotation Format

Novel chapters are plain Markdown files. Korean words are marked up with a simple inline syntax:

```
He walked joyonghi{조용히|adv|quietly|조용히 앉아요. — Sit quietly.} into the room.
```

This makes chapters easy to write, edit, and version control. Authors control exactly which words appear, when they appear, and what example sentence accompanies them.

## Who This Is For

- Readers who want to pick up Korean alongside a story they're already invested in
- Intermediate learners who find pure study tools too disconnected from real usage
- Anyone who's tried Duolingo, got bored, and wants something that feels like reading rather than homework
